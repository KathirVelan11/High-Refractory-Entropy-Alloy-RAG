# Refractory High-Entropy Alloys (RHEAs): A Short Primer

## Definition and concept

Refractory high-entropy alloys (RHEAs), also called high refractory entropy
alloys, are multi-principal-element alloys composed mainly of refractory metals
such as molybdenum (Mo), niobium (Nb), tantalum (Ta), tungsten (W), vanadium
(V), hafnium (Hf), zirconium (Zr), titanium (Ti), and chromium (Cr). Unlike
conventional alloys built around a single base element, RHEAs contain four or
more principal elements in near-equiatomic proportions. The original example,
reported by Senkov and co-workers in 2010, was the equiatomic MoNbTaW system.

## Phase formation and structure

Most RHEAs solidify into a single body-centered cubic (BCC) solid solution.
The high configurational entropy of mixing, on the order of R·ln(n) for n
equiatomic elements, helps stabilize a simple solid solution rather than
multiple intermetallic phases. Whether a single BCC phase forms is governed by
factors including the atomic size difference (delta), the enthalpy of mixing,
and the valence electron concentration (VEC). Low atomic size mismatch and a
near-zero enthalpy of mixing favor a stable, single-phase BCC solid solution.

## Mechanical properties

The defining attraction of RHEAs is retention of strength at very high
temperatures. MoNbTaW retains a yield strength of roughly 400 MPa at 1600 C,
far exceeding nickel-based superalloys, which soften above about 1100 C. This
makes RHEAs candidate materials for turbine blades, hypersonic leading edges,
nuclear reactor components, and other extreme-environment applications. The
trade-off is that many single-phase BCC RHEAs are brittle at room temperature,
with limited tensile ductility.

## Improving ductility and oxidation resistance

Two persistent challenges limit deployment: room-temperature brittleness and
poor oxidation resistance above 800 C. Adding elements such as titanium,
zirconium, and hafnium can improve ductility, while aluminum, silicon, and
chromium additions promote the formation of protective oxide scales. Alloys
such as HfNbTaTiZr (the "Senkov ductile RHEA") show appreciable room-
temperature plasticity, demonstrating that composition tuning can overcome the
intrinsic brittleness of the refractory BCC solid solution.

## Design and discovery methods

Because the compositional space is enormous, RHEA discovery increasingly relies
on computational tools: CALPHAD thermodynamic modeling, density functional
theory (DFT), and machine-learning surrogate models trained on existing data.
These approaches predict phase stability, density, and mechanical response,
narrowing the search before costly experimental synthesis by arc melting or
powder metallurgy.

## Key takeaways

- RHEAs are BCC solid-solution alloys of 4+ refractory elements.
- They keep high strength near and above 1600 C.
- Single-phase variants are often brittle at room temperature.
- Ti/Zr/Hf improve ductility; Al/Si/Cr improve oxidation resistance.
- Computational screening (CALPHAD, DFT, ML) accelerates discovery.
